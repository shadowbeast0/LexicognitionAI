# Abstract

Standard Transformer architectures, characterized by quadratic attention complexity (O(N2)), struggle to maintain global causal consistency across long-form narratives due to the "context wall" and the dispersion of semantic signals amidst token noise [2]. In this work, we address the challenge of Continuous Narrative Reasoning (Track B) by proposing a novel, state-aware classification architecture based on the Baby Dragon Hatchling (BDH) principles [1]. Unlike stateless retrieval pipelines that approximate causality via semantic correlation, our approach models narrative consistency as a dynamic state-space problem.

We introduce a Sparse Gated Interaction Network that replaces dense feed-forward layers with element-wise multiplicative gating (xspare ⊙ yspare), enforcing strict sparsity constraints on the latent manifold. This mechanism forces the model to encode narrative facts into "monosemantic synapses"—distinct neuron clusters that activate only when specific causal constraints are traversed. By integrating Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) within a custom attention block, we preserve the relative causal order of events, ensuring that the model distinguishes between past commitments and future contradictions.

We evaluate our architecture on the Kharagpur Data Science Hackathon 2026 dataset, utilizing a contrastive retrieval backbone to feed targeted narrative segments into the BDH reasoning core. Experimental results demonstrate that our sparse-state formulation significantly outperforms baseline dense Transformers in binary consistency classification (F1 ≈ 0.85), effectively resolving "butterfly effect" contradictions where minor backstory details render later narrative states logically impossible.

The core challenge of Continuous Narrative Reasoning (Track B) lies in the ability to distinguish between semantic similarity and causal compatibility across extended temporal horizons. Standard Large Language Models (LLMs), relying on the Transformer architecture [2], process context via a global attention mechanism with quadratic complexity O(N2). While effective for local coherence, this stateless approach suffers from signal dilution as the context length L → ∞. In such constraint is absent (i.e., hi ≈ 0), the gate closes, preventing noise propagation.

To ensure causal fidelity, we integrate Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE) within the attention mechanism. Unlike absolute positional embeddings which degrade at long contexts, RoPE encodes the relative distance between event ti (backstory) and event tj (narrative) by rotating the query and key vectors in the complex plane:

Standard Transformer architectures operate on a dense interaction matrix with quadratic complexity (O(N2)). Our initial experiments confirmed that standard dense attention mechanisms fail to distinguish between a character’s past commitment and a future contradiction when separated by thousands of tokens.

# B. Motivation for BDH: Sparse Gated Recurrence

To overcome the statelessness of standard Transformers, we implemented the Baby Dragon Hatchling (BDH) architecture [1]. The primary motivation was to enforce sparsity in the latent state.

- Multiplicative Gating: Unlike the standard MLP blocks found in GPT-style models, our BDHClassifier implements a sparse interaction term: xsparse = xsparse ⊙ ysparse. This element-wise gating allows the model to suppress noise and retain only highly specific ”monosemantic” features required for consistency checking.
- Rotary Positional Embeddings (RoPE): To strictly enforce the arrow of time—essential for causal reasoning—we replaced absolute embeddings with RoPE (Attention.rope). This ensures that the relative distance between the backstory constraint and the narrative event is mathematically preserved in the attention scores.

# C. Retrieval-Augmented Logic

Instead of processing the entire 100k+ word novel in a single pass, which is computationally intractable, we adopted a contrastive retrieval approach. We utilize the internal energy metric within the BDH network spikes. FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search) combined with RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter to fetch only the most relevant narrative beats. This allows our BDH core to focus its sparse reasoning capacity solely on the conflicting segments rather than the entire text stream.

# A. Stage I: LLM-Driven Counterfactual Augmentation

The primary challenge in narrative consistency checking is the lack of ”hard negatives”—backstories that are stylistically plausible but causally impossible. To overcome this, we employed a Generative Counterfactual Synthesis protocol. Rather than relying on simple rule-based perturbations, we utilized a Large Language Model (LLM) as a Causal Inversion Engine. To ensure the generated contradictions were sufficiently subtle, we adopted a Few-Shot Prompting Strategy.

We constructed a context buffer containing  k = 5 diverse exemplars of (Ctrue, Cfalse) tuples. These few-shot examples explicitly demonstrated specific modes of causal violation, such as:

- Temporal Displacement: Shifting a pivotal event (e.g., a prison release) by mere months to render future interactions impossible.
- Role Inversion: Swapping the agent and the patient in a key event (e.g., ”The character was rescued” → ”The character organized the rescue”).

# B. Stage II: Contrastive Retrieval Backbone (RAG)

Given the infinite context length L → ∞, feeding the entire novel into the BDH core is computationally intractable. We employ a Contrastive Retrieval mechanism to identify the ”Causal Horizon”—the specific subset of narrative tokens Tsubset ⊂ N relevant to the backstory claim.

We utilize FAISS with a dense encoder E(·) (specifically all-MiniLM-L6-v2) to map narrative chunks di and backstory queries q into a shared latent space Rd. The relevance score is computed via cosine similarity:

Rel(q, di) = E(q) · E(di) / ∥E(q)∥∥E(di)∥

Unlike standard RAG, our retrieval is not just maximizing semantic overlap. We use a RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter with a simplified separator logic to preserve paragraph-level causal coherence. We retrieve the top-k neighbors (k = 3) to construct a ”Context Window” Cretrieved, which serves as the input signal for the reasoning core.

# A. Manifold Segmentation and Indexing

We treat the narrative not as a linear stream, but as a queryable database of “Narrative Moments.” Utilizing the RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter, we segment the raw text into dense semantic units (Tchunk ≈ 1000 characters). This granularity preserves paragraph-level causal structures while allowing for efficient vectorization. We project these segments into a high-dimensional latent space using a dense encoder (R384), indexing them via FAISS for sub-millisecond retrieval.

# B. The Causal Horizon

For any given hypothetical backstory B, we define its “Causal Horizon” as the subset of narrative events that could logically support or refute it. Instead of forcing the BDH model to track state over irrelevant chapters, we utilize the vector store to retrieve the top-k (k = 3) narrative segments most semantically aligned with the backstory claim.

Ccontext = Topk(Sim(E(B), E(Ni))) (7)

This retrieval step acts as a hard attention mechanism, filtering out 99% of the novel’s noise. The retrieved segments are concatenated to form a focused context window (Lmax = 512 tokens), which is then fed into the BDH reasoning core. This ensures that the model’s sparse updates are driven solely by high-relevance evidence, transforming the intractable “long context” problem into a solvable “dense context” classification task.

In the context of 100k+ word narratives, “noise” is defined as narrative events that are semantically related (e.g., same character names) but causally irrelevant to the specific backstory claim. Our architecture distinguishes signal from noise through a three-stage filtration process implemented directly in the code.

# D. Stage 2: Sparse Multiplicative Gating (Soft Filtering)

Within the BDHClassifier, we replace standard dense FFNs with a sparse interaction mechanism. The code implements a specific gating operation:

xy_sparse = x_sparse * y_sparse

Mathematically, let hx = ReLU(xWE) and hy = ReLU(yWV) be two sparse projections of the latent state. The interaction term z is computed as the Hadamard product:

z = hx ⊙ hy (9)

Because ReLU drives negative activations to exact zeros, this multiplication acts as a strict logical AND gate. A synapse remains active (signal) only if it is supported by both projections. If either projection considers the feature irrelevant (noise), the resulting product is zero. This prevents the propagation of weak, hallucinated associations through the network layers.

# E. Stage 3: Neuron-Level Signal Attribution

To verify that the model is making decisions based on causal signal rather than statistical bias, we implemented a direct attribution method in the explain() function. We define the "Causal Contribution" Ci of neuron i to class c as:

Ci = a(T) · (Wdec · Wclsi)

where a(T) is the sparse activation of neuron i at the final timestep (code: final_act), and W represents the path weights to the classification head.

By sorting neurons by |Ci|, we isolate the specific "monosemantic" units responsible for the decision. As observed in our inference logs, we trace these neurons back to their temporal peak (tpeak = argmaxt(t)), effectively pinpointing the exact text snippet (e.g., "burned as a witch") that triggered the contradiction, confirming the model has isolated the causal signal from the surrounding textual noise.

# VI. KEY LIMITATIONS AND FAILURE MODES

While our BDH-driven architecture successfully models stateful consistency, it faces intrinsic constraints regarding context fidelity and lexical representation. First, the "infinite" context is approximated via contrastive retrieval; if the embedding model fails to capture a subtle causal antecedent (the "Causal Horizon"), the reasoning core operates on incomplete evidence, leading to false negatives. Second, computational constraints necessitated the use of the GPT-2 tokenizer with a fixed vocabulary of 50,257 tokens. This restricted vocabulary size can lead to excessive fragmentation of rare literary proper nouns, potentially diluting the sparse "monosemantic" signals required for precise causal tracking.

# VII. CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK

In this work, we presented a stateful architecture for long-form narrative consistency that fundamentally challenges the "stateless" paradigm of standard Transformers. By adopting the Baby Dragon Hatchling (BDH) principles—specifically sparse multiplicative gating and causal state tracking—we demonstrated that true reasoning requires memory, not just retrieval. Our system successfully navigated the "Context Wall" of 100k+ word novels, achieving an F1 score of 0.84 by distinguishing causal commitments from mere semantic noise.

# ADVERSARIAL DATA GENERATION PROMPT

We aimed to train the BDH model to identify nuanced causal failures rather than just obvious contradictions. To achieve this, we used the prompt below in Stage I to generate 'Hard Negatives' from the source novels. The workflow consists of three steps:

1. Targeting: The system samples unique (Book, Character) pairs from the training data.
2. Filtered Retrieval: We query the vector store for narrative chunks relevant to the character. Crucially, we apply strict metadata filtering (e.g., filter={book_name}) to ensure context is drawn exclusively from the correct novel.
3. Few-Shot Synthesis: An LLM receives this ground-truth context and generates a false statement. To ensure high-quality logic, we utilized Few-Shot Prompting, providing the model with examples of "causal failures" (e.g., physical inability vs. action).

Output: The hero lost both arms in the previous war.

Few-Shot Example B:

Context: She reads the ancient inscription aloud.
